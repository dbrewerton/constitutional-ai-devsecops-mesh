# Audit Evidence and Decision Trace Doctrine

**Status:** Proposed  
**Date:** 2026-07-20  
**Authority:** Subordinate doctrine under the Constitutional AI DevSecOps Mesh Constitution

## 1. Purpose

This doctrine defines how Human–AI collaborative systems preserve evidence so an independent reviewer can understand what was intended, what changed, what controls applied, who approved it, what evidence was produced, and what remained uncertain—without reverse-engineering the system from scattered logs.

The objective is not to make audits superficial. It is to make the truth easy to locate, reproduce, challenge, and defend.

## 2. Auditability as an architectural property

Auditability shall be designed into:

- repository structure;
- identifiers and timestamps;
- decision records;
- approval gates;
- deployment artifacts;
- synchronization and conflict handling;
- scanner evidence;
- environment separation;
- data retention and deletion;
- incident response.

Documentation created after the fact is not equivalent to evidence generated by the system at the time of the decision.

## 3. One authoritative source per concept

Each material concept should have one authoritative home and may be summarized elsewhere by reference.

Recommended hierarchy:

1. Constitution — highest policy authority.
2. Reusable doctrine — cross-product interpretation of constitutional principles.
3. Architecture Decision Record — why a durable decision was made.
4. Threat model and data-flow record — where risk and trust boundaries exist.
5. Implementation specification — how the product realizes the decision.
6. Automated evidence — tests, scans, build manifests, and generated artifacts.
7. Human approval record — accountable acceptance or rejection.
8. Operational record — deployment, event, incident, or conflict outcome.

A summary must link to its underlying evidence rather than silently duplicating and drifting from it.

## 4. Minimum trace chain

A material control or feature should be traceable through:

```text
Human intent
  -> constitutional principle
  -> doctrine
  -> ADR
  -> implementation requirement
  -> code/configuration
  -> automated test or scanner evidence
  -> human review
  -> deployment or operational artifact
  -> retrospective or incident evidence
```

A broken link is a finding or an explicitly accepted gap.

## 5. Evidence qualities

Evidence should be:

- attributable;
- timestamped;
- scoped;
- reproducible where practical;
- tamper-evident;
- minimally sufficient;
- understandable without hidden agent memory;
- linked to source revision and environment;
- retained under an explicit policy;
- distinguishable from opinion, inference, or recommendation.

AI-generated analysis must identify its source evidence, uncertainty, and whether a human accepted the conclusion.

## 6. Time and ordering

Distributed systems shall not treat high-resolution wall-clock timestamps as conclusive ordering evidence.

A defensible transaction trace should combine:

- durable server sequence or equivalent authoritative ordering;
- server receipt timestamp from a governed clock domain;
- revision ancestry or causal version information;
- device-reported wall time;
- device-local monotonic counter or nanosecond value where available;
- device and actor identity;
- idempotency key;
- content hash.

Clock precision is evidence about measurement granularity, not proof of correctness. Clock skew and delivery reordering must remain visible.

## 7. Competing changes and decisional trees

When concurrent or stale changes contain distinct information, the system shall preserve the alternatives rather than silently selecting a winner.

A decision artifact should retain:

- every candidate variant;
- provenance and revision ancestry;
- meaningful differences;
- content hashes;
- the authorized resolver;
- selection or merge mode;
- rationale;
- resulting revision;
- approvals;
- the canonical artifact hash.

The losing variant remains part of the historical record unless a lawful retention or privacy requirement mandates deletion or redaction. Such deletion or redaction should itself be recorded when permitted.

## 8. Segregation of environments

Development, test, and production are separate control domains.

Each environment should have independent:

- data stores;
- credentials;
- signing keys;
- payment modes;
- network boundaries;
- deployment identities;
- audit streams;
- configuration manifests;
- backup destinations.

No environment promotes itself. Promotion requires evidence from the source environment and accountable approval for the destination environment.

AI may prepare, analyze, and recommend a promotion. A human retains final accountable authority. A policy may require mutual Human–AI concurrence as a checks-and-balances gate, but absence of AI concurrence shall be represented as unresolved evidence—not disguised as human prohibition.

## 9. Administrative tooling

Operational tools such as container dashboards, database consoles, CI systems, and secrets managers are separate from the user-facing application.

They shall be:

- least-privileged;
- protected by a dedicated management plane;
- MFA-enabled where available;
- independently logged;
- disabled when not needed where practical;
- excluded from public application dependencies;
- treated as high-impact access paths.

An administrative convenience tool shall not become an undocumented bypass of production controls.

## 10. Audit map

A repository adopting this doctrine should maintain an audit trace map that identifies:

- control objective;
- constitutional principle;
- doctrine or ADR;
- implementation repository/path;
- automated evidence;
- human approval;
- operational artifact;
- current status;
- known limitation.

The map is an index, not a substitute for evidence.

## 11. Court and regulatory defensibility

A defensible system should allow an authorized representative to explain:

- the intended policy;
- how the policy was enforced;
- what the system knew at the time;
- what the human knew and approved;
- what the AI recommended and why;
- which evidence was contemporaneous;
- which conclusions were later interpretations;
- whether any control was bypassed;
- what corrective action followed.

The system shall not manufacture certainty, backdate evidence, silently rewrite history, or present an agent's confidence as legal fact.

## 12. Fraud and anomaly support

Audit evidence should support fraud and anomaly investigation without converting every user into a suspect.

Useful controls include:

- immutable or append-only high-risk action records;
- duplicate and replay detection;
- separation of requester and approver;
- unusual access and timing alerts;
- provenance of changed values;
- entitlement and payment reconciliation;
- conflict and override rationale;
- preservation of original and transformed artifacts.

Alerts are indicators requiring investigation, not automatic findings of misconduct.

## 13. Privacy and minimization

Auditability does not justify unrestricted retention.

Evidence collection shall be limited to what is necessary for security, accountability, contractual, operational, and legal purposes. Secrets, payment-card data, unrelated personal information, and excessive content snapshots are prohibited.

Retention, legal hold, redaction, access, and deletion rules must be explicit and auditable.

## 14. Constitutional test

Reviewers should ask:

1. Can the original human intent be located?
2. Can every material decision be traced to governing authority?
3. Can the implementation and exact source revision be identified?
4. Can automated evidence be reproduced or independently inspected?
5. Is human approval attributable and scoped?
6. Are timestamps supported by causal and sequence information?
7. Are competing changes preserved and their resolution explained?
8. Are development, test, and production segregated?
9. Are administrative tools prevented from becoming bypasses?
10. Are privacy and retention limits explicit?
11. Can a skeptical independent reviewer challenge the record?
12. Are known gaps visible rather than buried?

An unresolved answer keeps the control in proposed, partial, or exception status.

## 15. Reference implementation

DJ Intelligence is an early reference implementation through:

- authoritative synchronization sequences;
- idempotent mutations;
- conflict variants and resolution artifacts;
- Human–AI mutual approval gates;
- isolated development/test/production presence design;
- hosted payment boundaries;
- security evidence artifacts;
- product-specific ADRs and acceptance plans.

Product code and private data remain outside this governance repository.
